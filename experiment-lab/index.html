How experiments work — read once, applies to everything below
1
The split
Random group assignment
When a user arrives, we hash their user ID to assign them to a group — deterministically and permanently. 50% see the old version (control), 50% see the new one (test). The same user always sees the same version. Neither group knows they're in an experiment.
2
The measurement
Count successes per group
We define one success event upfront — e.g. "completed a purchase." We count how many users in each group succeeded. The difference between those percentages is the effect we're trying to measure.
3
The math check
Is it real or luck?
Any two groups differ slightly by random chance. A Welch's t-test calculates: "if there's truly no effect, how likely is it we'd see a gap this large by chance?" If that probability (the p-value) is below 5%, we call it statistically significant — it's probably real, not a fluke.
4
The safety check
Did we break anything?
We also track guardrail metrics — things we never want to harm, like page load speed or refund rate. If any guardrail regresses significantly, the experiment is blocked from shipping even if the goal metric looks great.

email_notifications
help_outline Question: Do daily digest emails bring users back to the app more often?
check_circle_outline Success = user opens the app within 24h of receiving the email
⚠
The trap
Day 7 looked like a clear win
At Day 7 the open rate delta was +8.7pp with p<0.001. Most teams would have shipped immediately. The result looked massive and highly significant. But something suspicious was happening in the time series.
⚠
What's actually happening
The delta is shrinking every day
By Day 14 the delta has fallen to +1.9pp and is still declining. Users opened the emails because they were new and curious. As novelty wore off, they stopped engaging. The "improvement" was curiosity, not genuine value.
⚠
The pattern
What novelty looks like
Novelty: large delta early → steadily shrinks → approaches zero. Real effect: stable delta throughout. Always run for at least 2 full use cycles (for a daily feature, that's 14 days minimum) and check the time series before deciding.
Delta (test − control) by day — novelty wearing off vs. what a real effect looks like
hourglass_empty Verdict: Do not ship yet — novelty effect detected
Daily digest emails appeared to increase app opens by +8.7pp at Day 7. But by Day 14 that delta has shrunk to +1.9pp and is still declining. Users opened emails out of curiosity about a new feature, not because the emails are genuinely useful.

What to do: run until Day 28 or until the delta stabilises. If it stabilises above zero, the effect is real but smaller than initially measured — make a ship decision based on that stable number. If it reaches zero, the emails provide no long-term value and should not be shipped.

The lesson: stopping experiments early (called "peeking") is one of the most common sources of false positives. The time series chart is your best defence — always check it.

Interaction effects — what happens when you ship two winners at once
Advanced
info
onboarding_flow_v2 and new_dashboard_layout both look good individually. The team wants to ship both simultaneously. But users who were in both experiments show an unexpected result.
D7 Retention lift by group
What the chart shows
  • Onboarding only: +1.1pp D7 retention (what the experiment measured in isolation)
  • Dashboard only: +0.8pp D7 retention (measured in isolation)
  • Expected if additive: +1.9pp (just adding the two)
  • Actual when both shipped: +2.6pp — more than the sum of parts. The features reinforce each other.
Why this matters and what to do
  • If you shipped them separately, you'd attribute +1.1pp to onboarding and +0.8pp to dashboard. You'd miss the +0.7pp interaction bonus entirely — and wouldn't know it existed.
  • To measure this properly: set up a holdout group that sees neither change. After 4–6 weeks, the delta between the holdout and everyone else captures the true combined impact of everything shipped.
  • Interactions can also be negative (two changes cancelling each other out). A holdout group catches both.

Experiment — multiple testing false positive + real regression
sidebar_redesign_v2
help_outline Question: Does a reorganised sidebar help users navigate to features faster?
functions 30 metrics tracked · 112,004 users/group · Day 21
⚠
The math problem
30 metrics = expected false positives
At 95% CI, 5% of all tests show "significant" by pure chance — even when nothing is happening. With 30 metrics: 30 × 5% = 1.5 expected random flukes. Seeing 1 green bar out of 30 is exactly what you'd expect from noise. It proves nothing.
⚠
The suspicious green bar
sidebar_click_rate +0.8pp
p=0.04, barely significant. But: (1) not pre-registered as a goal metric, (2) the CI barely clears zero — tiny daylight, (3) at 99% CI this result disappears entirely. A genuine effect at 112k users would be robust to threshold changes.
!
The real problem
content_consumption −2.1pp
This regression has large daylight and remains significant at 99.9% CI. Users in the redesigned sidebar are consuming less content. The navigation changes are making it harder to find things — the opposite of the goal.
p-value distribution across all 30 metrics
If the experiment truly had no effect, p-values would be uniformly spread across all buckets. This histogram shows most metrics at high p-values (gray bars), with just 2 outliers at p<0.05. That's exactly what you'd expect from random noise — not a real effect.
How to detect false positives
  • Pre-register your primary metric before the experiment. Post-hoc hunting through 30 metrics is p-hacking.
  • Check daylight: the green bar barely clears zero. At 112k users, a real effect would be obvious.
  • Raise CI to 99% when reviewing many metrics. Apply Bonferroni correction: require p < 0.05/30 = 0.0017 to trust any single result.
  • At 99% CI: the green bar vanishes. The red bar (regression) remains.
block Do not ship
1 green bar out of 30 = expected from chance. The only confirmed real signal is a regression in content consumption. The sidebar redesign is making navigation harder, not easier. The experiment has failed its goal.

What are company metrics?
The guardrails no one can skip
Company Critical Metrics (CCMs) are checked against every experiment automatically — regardless of what the experiment targets. If any CCM regresses, the experiment is blocked from shipping. Individual teams cannot override this. Metric owner sign-off is required for any exception.

!
The company metric
Daily active users −0.3%
Fewer unique users are opening the app each day. The algorithm optimises within a session — but makes the product less worth returning to. Users feel "done" with their feed and don't come back. This is the classic local-vs-global optimisation trap.

The personalised feed algorithm improves engagement within a session (clicks, likes, time spent). However, daily active users dropped −0.3% — meaning fewer users open the app each day. This is a company-level health metric and its regression blocks shipping, even though all product metrics look positive.

Why this rule is absolute: individual teams optimise for their own metrics. Without company-level guardrails, a team could make their feature look great while quietly eroding the platform's overall health. The DAU check prevents this.
